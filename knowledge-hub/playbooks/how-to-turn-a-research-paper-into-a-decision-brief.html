How to Turn a Research Paper into a Decision Brief

A decision brief translates strong research into the form decision-makers need: context, evidence, implications, limits, and practical next moves.

Domain Cross-Disciplinary Innovation Reading time 6 min read Level Practitioner

Playbook Purpose

What problem this playbook solves

Strong research often fails to influence action because it stays in the form in which it was produced. A paper can be careful, nuanced, and credible, yet remain unusable for people who need to make decisions, allocate resources, shape a pilot, or redesign a workflow.

A decision brief solves a translation problem. It reorganises the most decision-relevant parts of a paper for readers who need clarity more than academic form. This does not mean oversimplifying the work; it means translating it responsibly.

What a Decision Brief Must Answer

Five questions for a usable brief
Issue

What practical issue does this research speak to?

Evidence

What does the paper actually show?

Meaning

Why does the evidence matter in practice?

Limits

What should the reader be cautious about?

Options

What actions, pilots, or follow-up questions does it open?

Owner

Who is positioned to act or request the next step?

Six-Step Translation Process

Write for the decision, not for the paper.

  1. Define the decision contextClarify the choice, audience, and practical question before opening the paper.
  2. Extract the decision-relevant coreIdentify the problem statement, main finding, evidence boundary, and practical implication.
  3. Rebuild for a non-academic readerUse context, evidence, meaning, limits, and options rather than the paper’s original structure.
  4. Translate, do not flattenRemove avoidable complexity while preserving caveats and conditions.
  5. Add a decision layerTurn findings into options, pilot logic, no-go guidance, or monitoring requirements.
  6. Review for rigor and usabilityCheck whether the researcher would recognise the work and the decision-maker would understand it.
Use Case

When to use this playbook

This format works when the underlying research is strong but the audience is not looking for a full academic reading experience. It is especially useful for programme teams, innovation leads, department heads, policy partners, grant managers, and institutional decision-makers.

It is not a replacement for a technical review, systematic synthesis, or methodological replication. In those cases, the brief can sit beside the longer document, but should not pretend to contain it all.

Sample Decision-Brief Structure

1. Decision contextOne paragraph explaining the practical question and audience.
2. What the research examinedA short description of the study, evidence base, or method.
3. What it foundThe most decision-relevant result in plain language.
4. What this meansPractical implications for the target audience.
5. Limits and cautionsWhat the evidence does not justify yet.
6. Options or next stepsPossible actions framed clearly and proportionately.
Translation Quality Test

The brief is rigorous when

  • Necessary uncertainty remains visible.
  • Evidence type and boundary are clear.
  • The original researchers would recognise the claim.
  • The implications do not exceed the evidence.

The brief is usable when

  • A non-specialist can scan it quickly.
  • The decision context is explicit.
  • Options are clear but proportionate.
  • Next steps have owners or conditions.
Mistakes to Avoid

Common mistakes

One common mistake is confusing a decision brief with a summary note. A summary reproduces content; a brief supports a decision. Another mistake is stripping away all uncertainty to sound decisive. That weakens trust.

A third mistake is making the brief too long. If it recreates the density of the paper, it loses its value. The brief should be lean enough to support action and strong enough to protect rigor.

◎

Decision Brief Completion Check

If one answer is weak, revise before circulation.

  • Is the decision context clear?
  • Is the evidence accurately represented?
  • Are implications and limits both visible?
  • Are next steps proportionate to the evidence?
  • Can the target reader act without reading the full paper first?
