How to Run a Small AI-for-STEM Capability Session

A small AI-for-STEM session should build practical judgement about workflows, risk, evidence, and review rather than simply demonstrate tools.

Playbook Use

When to use this playbook

This playbook is for teams that want a focused introductory AI-for-STEM capability session without pretending that a short workshop will solve institutional readiness by itself.

The goal is not to impress people with tools. The goal is to help participants understand where AI can add value, where it introduces risk, and how responsible use should look in real STEM and technical workflows.

What Success Looks Like

A good session leaves participants more precise
Capability

Participants understand what AI is and is not good for in their context.

Workflow fit

They identify at least two task points where AI might genuinely help.

Risk awareness

They name boundaries around hallucination, source quality, confidentiality, and overreliance.

Evidence mindset

They distinguish generic generation from grounded, reviewable support.

Feedback

The host team gathers questions, concerns, and suggested use cases.

Next step

The session ends with a pilot, policy note, readiness review, or pause.

Prerequisites and Inputs

Before the session, define the audience: educators, researchers, students, technicians, programme staff, or mixed stakeholders. Prepare a short pre-session note, one or two realistic workflow examples, and a boundary statement that explains what the session does not cover.

  • One realistic workflow exercise.
  • A facilitation guide with timing checkpoints.
  • A boundary statement for risk and review.
  • A simple form for follow-up actions and support needs.

60–90 Minute Session Structure

Keep the session grounded in interpretation rather than tool theatre.

01Frame the purpose

Explain why the session is being held and what practical capability means.

02Teach AI basics

Focus on workflow logic, not model trivia.

03Show applied examples

Compare a strong use case with a weak or risky one.

04Run guided exercise

Ask participants to map AI support to a task they actually perform.

05Discuss boundaries

Cover hallucination, bias, source quality, confidentiality, and expert judgement.

06Set next steps

Capture use cases, governance questions, training needs, and pause conditions.

Example Design

How to choose examples

Examples make or break the session. The best examples are domain-relevant, bounded, and easy to discuss critically. Avoid relying only on creative-writing prompts or generic chatbot tricks.

Better examples include comparing a raw model answer with a retrieval-grounded answer over a known document set, turning a dense paper into a decision brief outline, or showing how a model can organise lab notes while still requiring human interpretation.

Practical Exercise Pattern

The exercise moves the conversation from abstract interest to practical discernment.

  1. Pick one recurring taskParticipants choose a task they perform regularly.
  2. Break it into stepsThey map the workflow from input to final use.
  3. Locate possible supportThey identify where AI could help with ideation, organisation, drafting, retrieval, or review.
  4. Locate no-trust zonesThey name final claims, uncontrolled citation, high-stakes judgement, and unsupported recommendations.
  5. Write one next actionEach participant leaves with a realistic change or question to carry forward.
Common Failure Points

Watch for

  • Trying to cover every AI concept in one short session.
  • Making the session too product-specific.
  • Ignoring different confidence levels in the room.
  • Ending without a next step.

Facilitate toward

  • Curiosity without hype.
  • Skepticism without dismissal.
  • Task, evidence, and context.
  • A small number of concrete follow-ups.
◎

After-Session Synthesis

The synthesis is where a workshop becomes capability-building.

  • Which immediate use cases are credible?
  • Which policy or governance questions surfaced?
  • What training needs were visible?
  • What issues should not proceed yet?
  • Is the next step a pilot, guidance note, readiness assessment, or pause?
