AI Adoption Readiness for Research Teams

A readiness model helps research teams move from tool enthusiasm to the conditions required for useful, safe, repeatable AI-assisted work.

Domain AI for STEM Innovation Reading time 7 min read Level Practitioner

Framework Purpose

What problem this framework solves

Many teams experiment with AI before they are ready to use it well. They may have access to tools, but lack clear use cases, good data habits, review workflows, and governance boundaries.

A readiness model helps teams slow down in the right way. It does not ask whether a team is enthusiastic. It asks whether the conditions exist for AI to be useful, safe, and sustainable.

Six Readiness Dimensions

A useful readiness model must be simple enough to apply and rigorous enough to guide decisions.

Problem fitThe team knows which workflow problem AI should improve and which tasks remain outside scope.
Data and knowledge qualityDocuments, datasets, notes, and source material are reliable, current, and organised enough to support use.
People and skillDomain experts, technical users, editors, and reviewers understand limits and validation duties.
Workflow designAI fits into repeatable intake, drafting, checking, approval, and storage steps.
Governance and assuranceResponsibility, restricted information, checking standards, and failure behavior are explicit.
Learning and improvementThe team collects feedback, errors, examples, and performance signals over time.

Scoring Logic

Rate each dimension before deciding scope
1 — Absent

The capability is informal, inconsistent, or missing.

2 — Emerging

A few practices exist, but ownership and quality are uneven.

3 — Partial

There is structure, but it depends on motivated individuals.

4 — Repeatable

The workflow is explicit, documented, and used more than once.

5 — Reviewed

The capability is monitored, improved, and explainable to stakeholders.

Pattern > total

A low governance score can block readiness even when enthusiasm is high.

Readiness Levels

What low, medium, and high readiness look like

Low readiness looks like casual tool exploration without a narrow problem, trusted source base, or review process. Outputs vary widely, and accountability is unclear.

Medium readiness means one or two promising use cases exist and some checking practices are emerging, but governance, documentation, or evaluation still depend too much on a few people.

High readiness means the team knows where AI fits, where it does not fit, what knowledge grounds it, who reviews it, and how performance is monitored.

Decision Gates

Readiness should lead to decisions, not just a maturity label.

Gate 1: Use case clarity

Do not launch until the team can state the exact workflow and user need.

Gate 2: Source quality

Do not ground outputs on documents that are stale, fragmented, or unowned.

Gate 3: Review owner

Do not publish or operationalise outputs without a named human review role.

Gate 4: Risk boundary

Do not include confidential, high-stakes, or restricted material without explicit controls.

Worked Example

A short worked example

Consider a research support team that wants AI for literature briefing, workshop preparation, and document querying. It may score highly on problem fit because tasks are clear. It may score moderately on people and workflow if staff understand the use cases but do not yet document procedures consistently.

If it scores low on data quality because documents are spread across email threads and cloud folders without version control, the next priority is not another tool. It is a better knowledge base and an explicit review process.

Common Mistakes

Avoid

  • Confusing tool access with readiness.
  • Over-scoring because of isolated success stories.
  • Assessing readiness without risk or governance.
  • Treating readiness as a one-time exercise.

Do instead

  • Use evidence for every score.
  • Repeat the assessment after a pilot.
  • Connect each score to one improvement action.
  • Document what remains out of scope.
◎

Before Moving Forward

If these questions cannot be answered clearly, the team is ready only for limited experimentation.

  • What problem are we solving?
  • What source base grounds the work?
  • Who reviews the output?
  • What information is restricted?
  • How do we know the workflow is working?
  • What will we improve next?

The practical takeaway: AI adoption readiness is less about access to tools and more about people, knowledge, governance, and repeatable work.
