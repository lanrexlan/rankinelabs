A Circularity Readiness Model for Infrastructure Decisions

Circularity readiness shifts the question from whether an organisation believes in circularity to whether it is positioned to make credible circular decisions.

Framework Purpose

What problem this framework solves

Circularity is now used in many infrastructure conversations, but the word often appears before the conditions for circular action exist. That creates ambitious language, weak pathways, and little clarity about where to start.

A readiness model shifts the question from “do we believe in circularity?” to “are we positioned to make circular decisions well?” That is more useful because circularity depends on design choices, procurement rules, material flows, supplier relationships, operations, and measurement habits.

Six Readiness Dimensions

Strategic intentCircularity is defined clearly enough to guide decisions rather than serve as broad aspiration.
Design and specificationTeams compare repair, reuse, modularity, maintenance, and end-of-life implications before options close.
Supply-chain and market fitSuppliers, recovery pathways, reverse logistics, or refurbishment options are credible in context.
Data and traceabilityMaterials, lifecycle histories, asset condition, and recovery pathways can be identified.
Governance and incentivesProcurement rules, budgets, contracts, and accountability structures support circular outcomes.
Measurement and learningTeams track whether circular interventions deliver value and adjust future decisions.

Four-Level Scoring

Use levels to support conversation, not precision theatre
Level 1

Circularity is mainly rhetorical; evidence and ownership are weak.

Level 2

Isolated efforts exist, but integration is limited.

Level 3

Circular choices are operational in selected workflows.

Level 4

Circularity influences design, procurement, operations, and review consistently.

Evidence rule

Every score should be backed by a concrete example or document.

Action rule

The lowest constraining dimension should become the next improvement target.

Application Logic

How to apply the framework

Start with one infrastructure decision, not a whole organisation. That could be a material substitution choice, asset replacement, building retrofit package, or maintenance strategy.

Score the six dimensions against that decision context. Then identify where the lowest-scoring dimensions are blocking the rest. Circularity often fails because one missing condition—procurement flexibility, traceability, supplier capability—quietly undermines the whole initiative.

Readiness Gate Sequence

01Frame the decision

Name the asset, material, or lifecycle choice under review.

02Score dimensions

Use evidence to rate readiness across the six dimensions.

03Identify bottleneck

Find the dimension that most constrains credible circular action.

04Set intervention

Choose the policy, data, design, or market action that improves readiness.

05Review after decision

Track whether the circular choice delivered value and what changed.

Worked Example

Worked example

Imagine a team considering recycled or polymer-enhanced material options in a refurbishment. Strategic intent may be strong because leadership wants lower waste and longer life. Design readiness may be limited if lifecycle comparison tools are absent. Supply-chain fit may be uncertain if recovery or sourcing partners are scarce.

The framework shows that the issue is not whether circularity is desirable. The issue is whether the surrounding conditions support a credible decision.

Common Misuse

Avoid

  • Turning readiness into branding.
  • Treating all dimensions as equally urgent in every context.
  • Ignoring local recovery infrastructure and supply-chain realities.
  • Using the score to claim maturity without evidence.

Use the model to

  • Create shared language for feasibility.
  • Reveal policy, market, data, or design bottlenecks.
  • Avoid overstating circular progress.
  • Decide whether to pilot, prepare, or pause.
◎

Before Calling a Decision Circular

A circularity claim is credible only when its conditions are explicit.

  • What value is being retained, and at which lifecycle stage?
  • Which mechanism retains that value: repair, reuse, remanufacture, refurbishment, or recycling?
  • What procurement or supply-chain condition must be true?
  • What data proves material identity, condition, or recovery pathway?
  • How will success be measured after implementation?
Reading Trail

References and further reading

Use this model alongside circular economy guidance for construction, infrastructure lifecycle analysis, material-passport thinking, procurement reform, and practical studies on circular barriers and drivers.

The practical takeaway: circular readiness is a decision-quality tool, not a marketing score.
